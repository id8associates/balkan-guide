# 15 Most Tourist Places in Bosnia and Herzegovina

## 1. Sarajevo — The European Jerusalem
**Region**: Sarajevo Canton (Central Bosnia)

**Description**: The capital and largest city of Bosnia and Herzegovina, Sarajevo is a fascinating blend of East and West. The old town (Baščaršija) is a vibrant Ottoman-era bazaar with cobblestone streets, copper workshops, mosques, and cevapi shops. The city is also marked by Austro-Hungarian architecture, the Latin Bridge (where WWI started), and the scars of the 1990s siege. Nicknamed the "European Jerusalem" for its historic coexistence of mosques, Catholic and Orthodox churches, and synagogues.

**Why Visit**: Rich history spanning Ottoman, Austro-Hungarian, and modern eras; exceptional food scene; affordable European capital; unique cultural mix found nowhere else. It won Foxnomad's "Best City to Visit" competition in 2012.

**Significance**: Host of the 1984 Winter Olympics; site of the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand triggering WWI; symbol of multicultural tolerance and post-war resilience.

**Things to Do**:
- Wander Baščaršija old town and visit Sebilj fountain
- Visit Gazi Husrev-beg Mosque (16th century)
- Explore the Tunnel of Hope (Sarajevo War Tunnel Museum)
- Ride the cable car to Trebević Mountain for panoramic views
- Visit the Latin Bridge and 1878-1918 Museum
- Try local food: ćevapi, burek, baklava at local restaurants
- Walk the eternal flame and Ferhadija pedestrian street

**Best Time to Visit**: May–September (warm, festivals). Also excellent March for city events and December–March for nearby skiing at Jahorina/Bjelašnica.

## 2. Mostar & Stari Most (Old Bridge)
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton

**Description**: One of Bosnia's most iconic destinations, Mostar is famous for its UNESCO-listed Stari Most (Old Bridge), a 16th-century Ottoman arched bridge rebuilt after its destruction in the 1993 war. The old town features cobbled streets, stone houses, artisan shops, and traditional restaurants along the Neretva River. The bridge is the stage for the famous local tradition of diving into the Neretva River.

**Why Visit**: The Stari Most is one of the most photographed landmarks in the Balkans; the old town's Ottoman atmosphere is beautifully preserved; excellent dining along the river.

**Significance**: UNESCO World Heritage Site (2005); masterpiece of Ottoman engineering built by Mimar Hayruddin in 1566; powerful symbol of reconciliation and rebuilding after the Bosnian War.

**Things to Do**:
- Walk across Stari Most and watch bridge divers
- Explore the Old Bazaar (Kujundžiluk) for copper crafts
- Visit Koski Mehmed Pasha Mosque (climb the minaret for views)
- Cross the river to the Croatian side for a different perspective
- Visit the War Photo Exhibition and Museum of War and Genocide Victims
- Day trip to Blagaj, Počitelj, and Kravice Waterfalls
- Dine at riverside restaurants along the Neretva

**Best Time to Visit**: May–October (April–May is ideal — spring flowers, pleasant temps, fewer crowds than July–August)

## 3. Kravice Waterfalls
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton (near Ljubuški)

**Description**: A stunning 25-meter-high, 120-meter-wide semicircular waterfall on the Trebižat River, often called "Bosnia's Plitvice." The waterfall cascades into a large turquoise pool perfect for swimming in summer. Surrounded by lush greenery and small restaurants serving local trout.

**Why Visit**: One of the most beautiful natural attractions in the Balkans; swimming allowed in the natural pool; much less crowded and cheaper than Croatia's Plitvice Lakes.

**Significance**: One of the largest waterfalls in Bosnia and Herzegovina; protected natural area; top summer swimming destination.

**Things to Do**:
- Swim in the natural pool beneath the falls
- Hike the trails around the waterfall complex
- Enjoy fresh trout at the on-site restaurants
- Visit nearby Kočuša waterfall (less crowded)
- Take photos from the lookout platforms
- Picnic in the surrounding green areas

**Best Time to Visit**: May–September (summer swimming — July/August best for water levels; spring for maximum waterfall volume)

## 4. Blagaj & Vrelo Bune
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton (12 km from Mostar)

**Description**: A historic village at the source of the Buna River (Vrelo Bune), one of the largest and most beautiful karst springs in Europe. The 16th-century Blagaj Tekke (Dervish monastery) is built into the cliffside right at the spring's mouth, creating a scene of extraordinary beauty. The emerald-green water and the mist rising from the cave are mesmerizing.

**Why Visit**: Arguably the most photogenic spot in Bosnia; the tekke is a rare example of Ottoman monastic architecture integrated into nature; the river source is crystal-clear and ice-cold.

**Significance**: UNESCO Tentative World Heritage site; 16th-century Dervish monastery; one of Europe's strongest karst springs (up to 43 m³/s); rich Ottoman heritage.

**Things to Do**:
- Tour the Blagaj Tekke (Dervish house interior)
- Walk along the Buna River spring boardwalk
- Visit the ruins of Stjepan-grad fortress above the village
- Try fresh river trout at the waterside restaurants
- Kayak or paddleboard on the Buna River
- Visit the old stone mill (now a museum)

**Best Time to Visit**: April–October (spring and fall for best photos with mist; summer for swimming in the river)

## 5. Počitelj
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton (near Čapljina)

**Description**: One of the most beautiful Ottoman-era fortified villages in the Balkans. Perched on a hillside along the Neretva River, Počitelj features a well-preserved medieval stone town with a clock tower (Sahat Kula), a mosque, an inn (han), and a madrasa (Islamic school) — all built between the 14th and 17th centuries. The village offers breathtaking views of the Neretva Valley.

**Why Visit**: A perfectly preserved Ottoman settlement frozen in time; stunning panoramic views; excellent photography opportunities.

**Significance**: UNESCO Tentative World Heritage Site (2007); founded by King Tvrtko I of Bosnia in 1383; classic example of Ottoman urban architecture.

**Things to Do**:
- Climb the Počitelj fortress for valley views
- Visit the Šišman Ibrahim Pasha Mosque (1563)
- Walk the cobblestone streets through the old town
- Explore the Gavran Kapetanović Tower
- Enjoy coffee at the traditional café by the river
- Visit local artists' galleries in restored stone houses

**Best Time to Visit**: April–October (spring and fall for comfortable climbing; summer can be very hot)

## 6. Jajce & Its Waterfalls
**Region**: Central Bosnia Canton

**Description**: A historic city built around an extraordinary 22-meter-high waterfall (Pliva Waterfall) that falls directly into the city center — one of the only waterfalls in the world located within a town. Jajce was the capital of the medieval Kingdom of Bosnia and also where Yugoslavia was founded in 1943 during the AVNOJ conference.

**Why Visit**: Unique waterfall-in-the-city experience; rich medieval and WWII history; beautiful Pliva Lakes nearby.

**Significance**: Capital of the medieval Kingdom of Bosnia; site of the 1943 AVNOJ conference where Tito's Yugoslavia was founded; the waterfall is a national monument.

**Things to Do**:
- View the Pliva Waterfall from multiple viewpoints
- Explore the Medieval Fortress of Jajce (13th-14th century)
- Visit the AVNOJ Museum (WWII history)
- Walk through the Mithraeum (2nd-century Roman temple)
- Relax at the Pliva Lakes (Great and Small Lake) just outside town
- Visit the Catacombs (underground church from the 15th century)
- Kayak or fish on the Pliva River

**Best Time to Visit**: April–October (spring for maximum waterfall volume; summer for lake activities; October for fall colors)

## 7. Una National Park
**Region**: Una-Sana Canton (near Bihać)

**Description**: A stunning national park protecting the Una River and its spectacular waterfalls, rapids, and emerald-green pools. The park's crown jewel is the Štrbački Buk waterfall, a 24-meter-high cascade. The Una River (name means "the one") is known for its extraordinary turquoise color and is considered one of Europe's most beautiful rivers.

**Why Visit**: Pristine nature with world-class waterfalls; excellent rafting and kayaking; much less crowded than comparable parks in Croatia or Slovenia.

**Significance**: National park protecting the Una River ecosystem; Štrbački Buk is one of the most beautiful waterfalls in the Balkans; important trout fishing destination.

**Things to Do**:
- Rafting on the Una River (Class II-IV rapids)
- Hike to Štrbački Buk waterfall
- Swim in the natural pools at Martin Brod
- Visit the Ostrožac Castle (16th-century fortress)
- Fly fishing for huchen and trout
- Kayak through the Una River canyons
- Explore the city of Bihać with its old town and cafes along the river

**Best Time to Visit**: May–September (rafting best May–June with high water; swimming best July–August)

## 8. Međugorje
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton

**Description**: One of the most important Catholic pilgrimage sites in the world. Since 1981, when six local children reported visions of the Virgin Mary, Međugorje has drawn millions of pilgrims. The town features several large churches, the Apparition Hill (Podbrdo), and Cross Mountain (Križevac), plus countless shops selling religious items.

**Why Visit**: Third most important Catholic pilgrimage site in Europe; powerful spiritual atmosphere even for non-religious visitors; affordable accommodation and dining.

**Significance**: Since 1981, over 30 million pilgrims have visited; officially approved for pilgrimage by the Vatican (though apparitions still under investigation); major economic driver for Herzegovina.

**Things to Do**:
- Walk up Apparition Hill (Podbrdo) — rosary path
- Climb Cross Mountain (Križevac) for sunrise
- Attend the evening prayer program at St. James Church
- Visit the adoration chapel
- Explore the surrounding wine country (Herzegovina wine region)
- Visit nearby ancient stećak tombstones
- Explore the town's many religious souvenir shops

**Best Time to Visit**: March–November (June–August peak pilgrimage season; August 15th (Assumption) is the largest gathering)

## 9. Trebinje
**Region**: Republika Srpska (southern Herzegovina)

**Description**: A beautiful Ottoman-era city on the Trebišnjica River, often called "the city of sun and platan trees." Trebinje is Herzegovina's southernmost city, sitting near the border with Montenegro. Its old town (Stari Grad) features stone houses, the Arslanagić Bridge, and the picturesque Hercegovačka Gračanica monastery perched on a hill.

**Why Visit**: Authentic, uncrowded Ottoman atmosphere; excellent wine region (famous vranac and žilavka wines); gateway to the Adriatic.

**Significance**: One of the oldest cities in the Balkans; center of Herzegovina's wine production; the last Bosnian city before the Adriatic coast.

**Things to Do**:
- Explore the Old Town (Stari Grad) with its stone streets
- Visit Hercegovačka Gračanica monastery (incredible views)
- Walk across the Arslanagić Bridge (16th century)
- Wine tasting at local wineries (Tvrdos Monastery, Vukoje)
- Stroll the Trebišnjica River promenade
- Visit the Tvrdos Monastery (15th century)
- Explore the Old Town mills along the river

**Best Time to Visit**: April–October (September–October best for wine harvest and comfortable temperatures)

## 10. Banja Luka
**Region**: Republika Srpska (northern Bosnia)

**Description**: The second-largest city in Bosnia and Herzegovina and the administrative center of Republika Srpska. Known as the "Green City" for its abundant parks, tree-lined boulevards, and the beautiful Vrbas River canyon. The city's main landmark is the Kastel Fortress on the riverbank, and it boasts the recently rebuilt Ferhadija Mosque, a masterpiece of Ottoman architecture.

**Why Visit**: Lively city with excellent cafe culture; natural beauty of the Vrbas River; rich Austro-Hungarian and Ottoman heritage; affordable.

**Significance**: De facto capital of Republika Srpska; home to the rebuilt Ferhadija Mosque (UNESCO candidate); major cultural and economic center of northern Bosnia.

**Things to Do**:
- Visit Kastel Fortress (Roman/medieval origin)
- See the Ferhadija Mosque (rebuilt, originally 1579)
- Raft or kayak on the Vrbas River through the canyon
- Walk through Park Mladeži (youth park)
- Visit the Museum of Republika Srpska
- Explore Banja Luka's Austro-Hungarian architecture
- Hike up Banj Hill for city views

**Best Time to Visit**: May–September (summer for Vrbas rafting; May and September for comfortable city exploration)

## 11. Sutjeska National Park
**Region**: Republika Srpska/Foča (southeastern Bosnia)

**Description**: The oldest and largest national park in Bosnia and Herzegovina, home to Maglić (2,386 m — the country's highest peak) and Perućica, one of the last two remaining primeval forests in Europe. The park also features the stunning Sutjeska River canyon and the massive Tjentište war memorial commemorating the 1943 Battle of Sutjeska.

**Why Visit**: Untouched wilderness with Europe's last primeval forests; challenging hiking; WWII history; extremely remote and uncrowded.

**Significance**: Hosts Maglić, Bosnia's highest mountain; Perućica is one of Europe's last remaining virgin forests (not entered without a ranger); site of the pivotal WWII Battle of Sutjeska.

**Things to Do**:
- Hike to Maglić peak (challenging 2-day trek)
- Guided tour of Perućica primeval forest
- Visit the Tjentište War Memorial
- Hike to Skakavac Waterfall (75 m high)
- Camping in the park
- Explore the Sutjeska River canyon
- Wildlife spotting (bears, wolves, chamois)

**Best Time to Visit**: June–September (July–August for peak hiking; avoid April–May for muddy trails; winter for experienced mountaineers only)

## 12. Travnik
**Region**: Central Bosnia Canton

**Description**: A historic town in central Bosnia, once the capital of the Ottoman Viziers of Bosnia. Known as the "City of Viziers," Travnik features a well-preserved old town, two restored clock towers (sahat kule), several mosques, and the birthplace of Ivo Andrić, Yugoslavia's only Nobel Prize laureate in literature (1961).

**Why Visit**: Rich Ottoman history; birthplace of Nobel laureate Ivo Andrić; beautiful mountain setting; excellent local cuisine (Travnik cheese).

**Significance**: Capital of the Ottoman Viziers of Bosnia (1699–1850); birthplace of Ivo Andrić, author of "The Bridge on the Drina"; famous for Travnik cheese and ćevapi.

**Things to Do**:
- Tour the Sulejmanija Mosque (Colored Mosque)
- Visit the Ivo Andrić Birthplace Museum
- Explore the Plava Voda (Blue Water) spring area
- Climb to the Stari Grad fortress ruins
- Visit the Sahat Kula (Ottoman clock towers)
- Try Travnik cheese at local markets
- Hike on nearby Vlašić Mountain

**Best Time to Visit**: May–September (summer for mountain hikes; December–March for skiing on nearby Vlašić Mountain)

## 13. Višegrad & Mehmed Paša Sokolović Bridge
**Region**: Republika Srpska (eastern Bosnia)

**Description**: A town in eastern Bosnia most famous for the UNESCO-listed Mehmed Paša Sokolović Bridge across the Drina River — immortalized in Ivo Andrić's Nobel Prize-winning novel "The Bridge on the Drina." The town also features Andrićgrad (a stone town built by filmmaker Emir Kusturica), inspired by medieval and Ottoman architecture.

**Why Visit**: See one of UNESCO's most evocative Ottoman bridges; literary history; the Drina River canyon is breathtaking.

**Significance**: UNESCO World Heritage Site (2007); masterpiece of 16th-century Ottoman engineering designed by Mimar Sinan; central setting for Andrić's Nobel-winning novel.

**Things to Do**:
- Walk across the Mehmed Paša Sokolović Bridge (1577)
- Explore Andrićgrad (Kusturica's stone town)
- Visit the Andrić Institute museum
- Take a boat ride through the Drina River canyon (second deepest in Europe)
- Rafting on the Drina River
- Visit the Dobrun Monastery nearby

**Best Time to Visit**: April–October (spring for river volume; summer for rafting; autumn for colors around the Drina canyon)

## 14. Jahorina & Bjelašnica — Olympic Ski Resorts
**Region**: Sarajevo Canton

**Description**: The two main mountains that hosted the 1984 Winter Olympics. Jahorina (1,916 m) is the largest ski resort in Bosnia, with 20 km of slopes, while Bjelašnica (2,067 m) offers steeper terrain and spectacular views of Sarajevo. Both have been revitalized with modern lifts and offer some of the best-value skiing in Europe.

**Why Visit**: Excellent skiing at a fraction of Alpine prices; Olympic history; stunning mountain scenery; summer hiking and mountain biking.

**Significance**: Hosted the 1984 Winter Olympic Games (Jahorina — women's alpine; Bjelašnica — men's alpine); hosted the 2019 European Youth Olympic Winter Festival.

**Things to Do**:
- Skiing and snowboarding (December–March)
- Hiking and trail running in summer
- Mountain biking on alpine trails
- Visit the Olympic bobsled track on Trebević
- Paragliding off Bjelašnica
- Enjoy mountain lodges with traditional Bosnian food
- Overnight stays at mountain hotels for stargazing

**Best Time to Visit**: December–March for skiing (January–February best snow); June–September for hiking and mountain biking

## 15. Neum — Bosnia's Only Coastal Town
**Region**: Herzegovina-Neretva Canton

**Description**: Bosnia and Herzegovina's only Adriatic coastal town, a narrow 20 km stretch of coast between Croatian territories. Neum features small pebble beaches, several large hotels, and a relaxed Mediterranean atmosphere. It's more affordable than neighboring Croatian coastal towns like Dubrovnik (just 60 km away).

**Why Visit**: Bosnia's only access to the Adriatic Sea; more affordable than Croatian resorts; quiet and family-friendly; excellent seafood.

**Significance**: Bosnia's only coastal town (20 km coastline); historically part of the Republic of Ragusa corridor; growing tourism center with a Mediterranean flavor.

**Things to Do**:
- Relax on Neum's pebble beaches
- Swim in the crystal-clear Adriatic
- Fresh seafood dining at waterfront restaurants
- Boat trips along the coast
- Visit the nearby ancient Roman villa at Mogorjelo
- Day trip to Dubrovnik, Croatia (1 hour away)
- Explore the Pelješac Peninsula wine region nearby

**Best Time to Visit**: June–September (July–August peak beach season; June and September for fewer crowds and pleasant temperatures)

## Bonus: Lukomir — Europe's Highest Inhabited Village
**Region**: Rakitnica Canyon, Bjelašnica mountain

**Description**: The highest permanently inhabited village in Bosnia (1,495 m), Lukomir offers a glimpse into traditional Bosnian mountain life that has remained largely unchanged for centuries. Stone-roofed houses, sheepherding, and stunning views of the Rakitnica River canyon.

**Why Visit**: Authentic traditional village life; incredible canyon views; unique stone-roof architecture.

**Significance**: Highest village in Bosnia; living museum of medieval Dinaric lifestyle; on hiking routes from Bjelašnica.

**Best Time to Visit**: June–September (village is accessible and shepherds are present; winter is inaccessible due to deep snow)
